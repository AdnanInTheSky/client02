"Rising Solar Radiation: An Emerging Environmental Risk in Bangladesh”

Monday, March 10, 2025 YCI Team

As the sun rises over Bangladesh, its golden light paints a picture of warmth, life, and vitality. But behind this familiar glow lies an invisible force that is quietly becoming a serious environmental and public health concern: intensifying solar radiation. What was once simply sunlight is now transforming into a silent threat, exposing millions to increasing levels of ultraviolet (UV) radiation and extreme heat stress.

Bangladesh, located in the tropical belt, already receives high levels of solar exposure throughout the year. With climate change altering atmospheric conditions and thinning natural protective layers, the intensity of solar radiation has increased, especially during summer and pre-monsoon seasons. In Dhaka, UV Index (UVI) levels frequently reach 10–12 during peak daylight hours, placing the city in the ‘Very High’ to ‘Extreme’ risk categories, where unprotected exposure can cause biological harm within minutes. What was once routine outdoor life is now a slow accumulation of long-term health risks.

The danger is not visible, but its impacts are deeply biological. Prolonged exposure to UV radiation damages skin cells, weakens the immune system, accelerates skin aging, and significantly increases the risk of skin cancer and eye diseases such as Cataracts. For outdoor workers, such as farmers, rickshaw pullers, construction laborers, traffic police, and street vendors, solar radiation is not an abstract concept; it is a daily occupational hazard.

The causes of rising solar exposure are both global and local, and they must be clearly distinguished. At the global atmospheric level, climate change and long-term ozone layer stress reduce the atmosphere’s natural filtering capacity, allowing more harmful UV radiation to reach the Earth’s surface. At the local urban level, however, the dominant driver is structural: deforestation, shrinking green cover, rapid urbanization, and the loss of natural shade have removed protective ecological buffers. Concrete-heavy cities trap heat and reflect radiation, creating artificial thermal environments that intensify ground-level exposure.

Urban centers like Dhaka experience a compounded effect through urban heat island dynamics, where built surfaces absorb and retain heat, making cities significantly hotter than surrounding rural areas. This localized heating intensifies radiation exposure and produces chronic heat stress. The consequences extend beyond discomfort as dehydration, heatstroke, cardiovascular strain, and declining labor capacity become systemic risks. Research across tropical regions shows that for every 1°C rise in ambient temperature, outdoor labor productivity can decline by 1-2%, a serious economic threat in a country where millions depend on outdoor work for survival.

Yet, solar radiation is not only a threat. It is a powerful resource too. Bangladesh stands at a crossroads between solar challenges and solar potential. With one of the highest solar exposure rates in South Asia, the same radiation that harms health can fuel a clean energy transition. Expanding rooftop solar, solar irrigation systems, and renewable energy infrastructure can turn environmental vulnerability into sustainable opportunity.

Experts emphasize that protection and adaptation must go hand in hand. Urban planning must integrate tree corridors, green roofs, reflective building materials, shaded walkways, and climate-resilient architecture. Public health policies must prioritize UV awareness campaigns, protective clothing, sunscreen access, and heat safety guidelines for outdoor workers. Schools, workplaces, and transport systems must adapt to rising thermal stress conditions.

At the community level, restoring green spaces, protecting wetlands, and expanding urban forests can naturally regulate radiation and temperature. Climate education is equally critical as understanding invisible threats is the first step toward resilience.

Solar radiation is not an enemy, rather it is a force of nature. But unmanaged, unprotected, and unequal exposure transforms it into a silent crisis. Bangladesh’s future depends on whether it continues to absorb this invisible fire unprepared or learns to harness its power safely, sustainably, and equitably.

From survival to sustainability, the sun will define our future. The choice is whether it becomes our shield or our burden.
