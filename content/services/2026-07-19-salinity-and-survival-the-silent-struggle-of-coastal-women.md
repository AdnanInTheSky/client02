Climate change and gender discrimination accompany each other, especially in under-developed and developing countries. Similarly, in Bangladesh, climate change induced hazards**** like rising sea levels, cyclones, tidal flooding, and salinity intrusion are not only environmental issues, they are also social justice issues affecting the physical, mental, socio-economic, and cultural aspects of the coastal female community differently and intensely than those of the males. And this variation in impacts generates gender-biasness within the victims.

 

Salinity intrusion in the southwest coastal regions of Bangladesh is one of the most important hazards and its consequences are not gender-neutral. The most immediate impact of salinity intrusion is water insecurity. As ponds, tube wells become saline, women have to travel further to collect drinking water as part of their household maintaining responsibilities. Getting adequate drinking water, which is a very casual task for women around the country, is the hardest task for the coastal women. In many villages, women need to travel several kilometers carrying 15-20 liters containers multiple times a day. This continuous movement devours plenty of time from their daily life creating severe **time poverty**. As a result, women in coastal areas are less accessible of paid work, education, entertainment, rest, and overall personal nourishment.

 

Along with creating immense time poverty, it creates **physical burdens** such as back pain, musculoskeletal stress, exhaustion etc. And to highlight the gender-based biased in impacts, continuous consumption of saline water and lack of adequate hydration leads to reproductive and menstrual health complications. Women of the coastal areas face menstrual discomfort, hormonal imbalance, urinary tract infections, reproductive tract complications. Additionally, it contributes to higher underweight rates in **women (32.5%)** compared to **men (15.2%),** impacting the physical health status of women and adolescent girls living in coastal areas (Yasmin et al., 2026).  The same study reported 44.3% of women experienced menstrual health problems. These physical challenges even lead to gender-based violence in some cases. A study of 2019 shows 91.82% of coastal girls claim that salinity-induced skin problems are the key responsible factor for making their marriage unsecured.  

 

Besides physical challenges, what remains almost in dark is their **mental struggle and psychological distress**. The stagnant thought of collecting water for everyday household necessities despite complications in time, distance, and physical labor creates intense pressure on their mental health. The stress, anxiety, and fear haunt them every day. Recent studies have frequently reported psychological distress linked to water scarcity, livelihood instability, and caregiving pressure in coastal women and how the levels vary between men and women while women facing more of them than men.

 

Despite physical and mental constraints, women also endure **socio-economic pressure** due to salinity intrusion induced income loss, food insecurity. In many cases, men migrate to urban areas for earning and the overall household responsibility is placed entirely upon the women. Women do not have enough control over family resources despite being the ones to manage the household. In some cases, food insecurity leads to low consumptions among the females of the family saving as much as food for the males. Overall, this creates the **feminization of survival**- women carrying on greater economic, domestic, and emotional responsibilities without having greater power or resources.

 

So, along with climatic adaptation, the policymakers, researchers, and communities must take **women-led adaptation** into account, recognizing that climate solution is more fruitful when women participate in planning and decision-making. Investment in safe water infrastructure for coastal communities, inclusion of women in climate policy and local decision-making, expansion of reproductive and maternal health services should be ensured to eradicate both the salinity induced challenges and its gender-biased impacts on coastal women.
