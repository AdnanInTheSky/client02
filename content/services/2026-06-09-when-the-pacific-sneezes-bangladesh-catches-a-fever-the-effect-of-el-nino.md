Somewhere in a Joypurhat paddy field, a farmer is paying 25% more (from his savings or a loan) just to water his rice crop. Yet, when the monsoon finally arrives, the unpredictable weather patterns often leave crops waterlogged or destroyed.

 Tanvir, a mid-level corporate executive commuting from Mirpur to Motijheel, faces a different version of the same nightmare. During the punishing heatwaves, Tanvir’s daily commute becomes a grueling survival test. Stepping out at 8:30 AM, he is hit by a wall of humid heat. The city's concrete and dense traffic act like a furnace, radiating heat that leaves him exhausted before his workday even begins.

 But when the volatile monsoon finally breaks, it brings a different chaos. Within an hour of a heavy downpour, Dhaka’s clogged drains choke. Leaving his office at 6:00 PM, Tanvir finds Motijheel knee-deep in filthy, waterlogged streets.

 Whether drained by drought in the fields or drowned by the city’s broken infrastructure, El Niño spares no one. Thousands of kilometers away, warm water is pooling in the Pacific Ocean, and the connection of these two stories is not a coincidence. Every few years, a large part of the Pacific Ocean gets unusually warm. This natural event is called El Niño. Even though it starts in the ocean, it changes weather patterns worldwide. It mixes up where it rains and where it stays hot.

Now, Bangladesh, which is a very flat country with a lot of rivers, has some adversaries. Because of its shape and location, these weather changes hit the country quickly and cause severe problems.

Every few years, the weakened winds over the Pacific Ocean allow warm water to drift eastward. This event is called El Niño. El Niño is the warm phase of the El Niño-Southern Oscillation (ENSO) cycle. It occurs when the prevailing trade winds blowing west across the Pacific weaken, allowing warm surface water to drift eastward toward South America.

Even though this process begins thousands of miles away, it significantly disrupts the South Asian monsoon. This disruption is critical because Bangladesh relies on the vital rainfall that occurs between June and September for its agricultural output. El Niño typically suppresses this monsoon rain, leading to prolonged dry spells and heatwaves. On the flip side, its sister event, La Niña, represents the cold phase of the cycle, often bringing enhanced monsoon rains and severe, widespread flooding to the region.

 The current situation demands urgent attention. According to NOAA’s Climate Prediction Center, there is an 82% chance of El Niño conditions emerging in the May–July window, with a 96% probability that these conditions will persist through the Northern Hemisphere winter.

 The World Meteorological Organization (WMO) has issued a matching global warning, noting an 80% likelihood of an active El Niño event establishing itself globally between June and August. The WMO says that subsurface waters across the tropical Pacific have spiked more than 6°C above average, creating a massive reservoir of ocean heat. Because Bangladesh is low-lying and dense with river systems, these climate swings threaten immediate food security and the livelihoods of millions.

​Bangladesh is bracing for El Niño’s dual punch: punishing heatwaves and unpredictable rains. With the stakes high, preparation looks very different depending on where you live, especially in urban areas.

Our concrete cities trap heat because of what is known as the "urban heat island effect," which turns up the temperature for city dwellers. We can manage this urban heat problem adopting these: 

1. Beat the heat: Limit strenuous outdoor activity during peak hours, typically between 11:00 AM and 3:00 PM. Move to the coolest room in your home and wear loose, light-colored, breathable cotton clothing to help your body cool itself down naturally.
2. ⁠Smart hydration: Stay properly hydrated by drinking 2.5 to 3 liters of clean, safe water daily to replace what you lose from sweat.
3. ⁠The Night Paradox: While we usually worry about daytime heat, concrete walls and asphalt soak up heat during the day and radiate it back out at night. This keeps nighttime urban temperatures uncomfortably high and gives our bodies little time to recover.
4. Preparation for rain: Clear out neighborhood drains before the heavy monsoons hit so that you can reduce sudden, crippling waterlogging on local streets.
5. ⁠Preventing dengue: As the erratic rain-and-drought cycles create isolated pockets of stagnant water, communities must aggressively clear standing water from flowerpots, discarded tires, and open containers every few days to disrupt mosquito breeding.
6. ⁠Water safety: All drinking water must be brought to a rolling boil for at least 10 minutes or treated with water purification tablets before consumption to prevent diseases like diarrhea and cholera.
7. ⁠Emergency storage: Keep a basic supply of dry food rations, essential medicines, and oral rehydration salts (ORS) on hand in case waterlogging traps you indoors.

Last but not the least, a temperature fluctuation in the Pacific Ocean can disrupt an entire year of economic progress half a world away. Because individual preparedness can only do so much, tackling structural vulnerabilities requires shifting from reactive crisis management to long-term climate resilience. Bangladesh must prioritize drought-resilient crops to protect food security, upgrade urban drainage to prevent catastrophic waterlogging, and scale tree-planting initiatives to break up concrete heat sinks.
